Different types of eyelid surgeries

What is transconjunctival lower blepharoplasty pictures

There are multiple approaches to lower eyelid surgery. With the transconjunctival approach, an incision is made on the inside of the lower eyelid (pink portion) to access the fat. This works well for patients who have significant bulging of the fat in the lower lid without excessive skin laxity. This has been found to decrease the risk of the lower eyelid falling down after surgery.

The alternative is to approach the fat on the outside with an incision made through the skin below the lower lashes. This works well for patients who have significant skin laxity or redundancy and truly need an eyelid “lift”. There is a higher risk of the lower eyelid drooping after surgery, but aesthetically, if performed properly, will result in a refreshed, less tired appearance. ( Etai Funk, MD , Houston Facial Plastic Surgeon)

Better to Under- Than Over do Upper Eyelid Surgery

Tear production diminishes naturally as we age. The function of the eyelids, in addition to protecting the cornea, is to help spread the tear film over the eye and prevent exposure problems.

Therefore, over-excision of skin in a patient with mild dry eyes will lead to major league dry eyes. The good news: it is a very avoidable problem. My preoperative discussion of upper lid Blepharoplasty in Knoxville always begins with the possibility of incomplete eyelid closure (lagophthalmos). However, proper precautions make this a very rare event. The process is rather simple via preoperative marking with an awake patient in the upright position, known as the “pinch technique”. The upper eyelid crease is usually the lower extent of the incision. The skin of the upper eyelid is then sequentially pinched until it is determined how much skin would impede the upper lid from closing.

The upper extent of skin to removed is usually marked at 10-20% less than this, depending on the patient.

Most patients will have mild (1-2 mm) of lagophthalmos immediately after surgery related to weakness and/or local anesthetic effect on the orbiclaris oculi muscle; this wears off quickly. (Stephen Prendiville, MD, Fort Myers Facial Plastic Surgeon)

Eyelid closure after eyelid surgery is an important consideration

Eyelid closure after eyelid surgery is an important consideration. When removing excessive upper eyelid skin and/or fat from the upper eyelid areas, it is important that your surgeon not remove too much skin or this can affect your ability to close your eyelids resulting in chronically dry eyes.

This can be avoided by minimizing the amount of skin that is removed and making sure that you can still close your eyes when your physician performs the measurement for the skin removal. If you already have dry eyes then I would be even more conservative to make sure that extra skin is left behind. (Scott Trimas, MD, Jacksonville Facial Plastic Surgeon)

Do eyelids stay open after eye surgery

Following eyelid surgery normally the eyelids should close completely when the procedure is finished. The most common reason that they will not close following is due to too much skin has been removed from the upper eyelid. The second most common reason is that there may abnormal swelling or bleeding under the upper eyelid skin which should recede in a few weeks following surgery. If the inability to close the upper eyelid persists more than 3 to 4 weeks, an ophthalmologist or your surgeon should be consulted. If you experience a dry eye following upper eyelid surgery, you should begin using eye drops such as Systane or Restaxsis til the condition improves. If the conditiion persists past a month, the possibility of a skin graft to the upper eyelid may be necessary. (Gary H. Manchester, MD (retired), San Diego Plastic Surgeon)
