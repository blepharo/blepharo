Swelling after ANY surgery

Surgical operations are done by making cuts and re-arranging tissues. This process disrupts the normal lymphatic flow in the region, and until that heals, you will see swelling around the site of the operation. Post operative swelling from the “trauma” of the operation peaks at 48-72hrs, and then subsides

Swelling related to the lymphatic re-arrangement persists a long time after surgical repair of eyelid (up to a year or longer).

With operations that create big changes (breast augmentations, tummy tucks), most patients will not notice the swelling after a few months (since the change was so big, 1mm of residual swelling will not be noticed)

Operations that involve very thin tissues (eyelids) or regions that are meticulously inspected after a finesse operation for small changes (rhinoplasty), swelling will be noticed for MUCH longer (a year or more). Lymphatic swelling is more noticeable after increasing your cardiac output (cardio exercise) and is pressure / gravity dependent

(i.e.: if you had a face lift, sleeping on one cheek 3 weeks after the operation will lead to a flat region where the cheek was on the pillow (pressure), and a puffy region in front of this (gravity pooling fluid) compared to the other side where gravity helped drain away the fluid

Ptosis surgery and blepharoplasty are different procedures.

Just to be clear, there is brow ptosis (sagging of the eyebrows) which can cause pseudodermatochalasis of the upper eyelids (dermatochalasis is excessive or sagging eyelid skin), and there is eyelid ptosis, which is droopy eyelid

The latter requires levator surgery to lift and tighten the eyelid to the proper position. The former requires forehead lift to move the brows out of the upper eye socket to the orbital rim (for a man), or just above the orbital rim (for a woman)

Then there is true dermatochalasis of the upper eyelids, which is treated by blepharoplasty in Newport News. Each of these three operations is different, though blepharoplasty and levator surgery are both done through the same upper eyelid incisional approach
