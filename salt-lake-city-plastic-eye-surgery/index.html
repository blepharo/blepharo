What a blepharoplasty does/how it works

Blepharoplasty is surgery on the upper and/or lower eyelids. It is done to rejuvenate the eyelids and give a more youthful appearance.

The upper eyelids typically have skin removed and most of the fat retained, except in cases of very excess areas of fat.

The fat is retained so when the skin is closed a smooth appearance of youth is produced in the upper lid. The lower eyelid surgery is usually done to remove excess skin and the “bags” under the eyes. This can be done by an incision outside on the eyelid skin or inside the eyelid. Fat is removed to smooth the eyelid and excess skin resected to tighten the loose skin of the lid. (Jeffrey M. Darrow, MD, Boston Plastic Surgeon)

Blepharoplasty transconjunctival

Transconjunctival blephoraplasty is a wonderful procedure for the lower eyelid because it is very safe and protects the lower eyelid muscle.

It is fairly simple and works well to remove the fat “bags” of the lower eyelid. Transconjunctival means the incision to approach these fat “bags” is made on the inside of the eyelid.

Often, I will combine this with a “skin pinch” technique to remove extra skin on the outside of the lid. Doing this can help avoid a “pulled down” or sad appearance to the eyelid post operative.

The lower eyelid is a delicate structure and very important to approach with care. Be sure to consult with a board certified surgeon. ( Paul Watterson, MD , Columbus Facial Plastic Surgeon)

Lower Eyelid Blepharoplasty

Lower eyelid blepharoplasty can be performed through a lower eyelid incision or through a transconjunctival approach. Both of these incisions are associated with advantages and disadvantages when performing blepharoplasty. The lower eyelid incision is placed just below the eye lashes where it’s easily hidden.

This approach can be utilized to remove both excess lower eyelid skin and fat deposits. The procedure is associated with excellent results and high levels of safety, but must be used with caution when patients have laxity of the lower eyelid. The transconjunctival approach utilizes an incision that’s made on the inside of the eyelid where it’s not visible. This is an excellent approach when the patient has lower eyelid fat deposits with relatively tight skin. Under these circumstances, the fat can be removed without making a visible incision on the lower eyelid.

It’s also an excellent choice when lower eyelid laxity is present. When performing blepharoplasty surgery the choice of incision is dependent upon a variety of factors.

Careful analysis of the patient’s unique anatomy and aesthetic goals are necessary to determine which incision is best for any particular patient. The best way to accomplish these goals is to consult a board certified plastic surgeon with experience performing eyelid surgery. ( Richard J. Bruneteau, MD)

Salt Lake City Blepharoplasty transconjunctival – Inner Eye Bag Approach

The approach described as transconjunctival implies that the incision to access the fat pad is made inside the eye surface of the lower lid as opposed to through the skin. It allows for contouring of fat, removal of fat, repositioning of fat without crossing through the very fine orbicularis oculi musculature that lies on top of the fat pads. Essentially contouring of the eyelid can be addressed, but excess skin and skin texture must be addressed though other means.

Advantages are the following:

  1. No external incision is required.
  2. The risk for changing the function or position of the lower eyelid is less.
  3. There may be significantly less swelling and bruising.
  4. Autologous fat grafting and fat re-injection is still available as an option against the deeper bone and the tear trough.

Disadvantages are the following:

  1. Excess skin must be addressed through a separate external incision. (Keep in mind that the muscles around the eye in this case are not disturbed)
  2. The pocket may be more slightly more tight making fat repositioning into the tear trough slightly more technically challenging.
  3. Your surgeon has no mechanism to address a weakly suspended lower eyelid.
  4. Skin textural changes must be addressed with a separate procedure.
