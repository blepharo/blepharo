Eyelid surgery while awake?

Upper eyelid surgery is very commonly done under local anesthesia, even with ptosis repair In fact, upper eyelid surgery is the easiest procedure we do in a facial plastic surgery practice. Repair of the most common type of ptosis is accomplished with the same local anesthesia and it is recommended that you be somewhat cooperative at least so that the surgeon can measure the degree of correction of the ptosis.

It is impossible to do under general anesthesia. A mild sedative like Valium or Ativan can calm your nerves while the local anesthesia is placed, but once it is numb, you won’t feel anything.

The transconjunctival lower blepharoplasty with removal of lower eyelid fat can be a fantastic option for the right person. The key with any surgery is case selection. So a young person with just a bit of a lower eyelid bulge will have a great results with a small resection of lower fat using this approach

However, take too much fat or in individuals with a whole lot of lower eyelid issues like skin and eyelid laxity, cheek descent, etc and now you have a situation that will not do well with this approach

Conserving the lower eyelid fat is helpful when there is enough lower eyelid fat to be rotated into the top of the cheek in association with a hollowness in the under eye area. Again the key is individual selection

Generally individuals with thicker skin and good integrity of the lower eyelid ligaments do best with this type of procedure. Bottom line is that not every approach is right for every situation. The choice is much more art than science

‘Bulges’ Along Incision Line After Upper Eyelid Surgery Normal

After only nine days it is not unusual to see some fullness along an incision line.

Depending on the suture technique, sometimes there is localized swelling between the skin edge and the stitch which causes it to bulge up a bit.

This is normal in most cases. Fortunately, as time goes on, this in most cases goes away. Sometimes, we may recommend massage but care must be taken not to open up and disrupt the skin incision at this early time.

Follow your surgeon’s instructions with regards to this.

There are more than one specific specialty that performs blepharoplasty

I understand your dilemma. There is a significant amount of mis-guided and misinformation on the internet about what type of plastic surgery specialist to have do your procedure. There are a few factors to consider here.

First off, you should ask the surgeon how many of the particular procedure you are seeking, he or she performs per week or month or year. If they only do that procedure occasionally, that should be taken into consideration. You should also see at least 10 or more before and after photos of that procedure performed by that surgeon and ask if the results you are looking at are typical of his or her work.

Ask that surgeon what his or her revision rate is (how often does he or she need to touch up the surgery). Now realize that there are many fine General Plastic Surgeons around. Some may even perform mostly facial surgery.

In many cases however, the general plastic surgeon spends a significant amount of their time performing body surgery e.g. tummy tucks and breast surgery. On the other hand, there are also Facial Plastic Surgeons that are originally head and neck surgery trained (ENT) that may only, daily, perform facial plastic surgery.

In addition, Oculoplastic Surgeons are Ophthalmology trained physicians that then also complete an Oculoplastic surgery fellowship in plastic surgery of the eye and surrounding region. With the latter two specialties, both the Facial Plastic Surgeon as well as the Oculoplastic Surgeon may be better versed than the Plastic Surgeon who operates from head to toe.
